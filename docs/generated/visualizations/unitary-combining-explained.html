A unitary is a quantum operation that is reversible and preserves probability. Every operation on a quantum computer must be unitary — it's a law of quantum mechanics.
Example: Pauli-X (a single Pauli string of length 1)
X = [ 0 1 ]
    [ 1 0 ]
X × X† = I ✓ reversible
preserves |α|² + |β|² = 1 ✓ probability
Every Pauli string is already a unitary. XXYY is a unitary. ZZXX is a unitary. IIII is a unitary. Each one can be applied as an operation on the quantum computer and measured. The problem isn't that they're not unitaries — it's that there are too many of them.
The Hamiltonian is a sum of Pauli strings with coefficients:

H = 0.35·(XXYY) + 0.18·(YYXX) − 0.22·(XXZZ) + 0.41·(ZZXX) + ...

To find the molecule's energy, you need the expectation value ⟨H⟩ — the weighted average of measuring each Pauli string. The question is: how many separate measurements do you need?
Without grouping: You measure each Pauli string separately. Each measurement requires its own quantum circuit, its own set of repeated shots, and produces one expectation value.
Measuring 6 Pauli strings one-by-one
6 strings → 6 separate measurement rounds. Each round needs ~1000 shots for statistical accuracy. Total: 6,000 quantum operations. And this is just 6 strings — real molecules have millions.

How does one measurement give multiple expectation values?

When you combine anticommuting P₁ and P₂ into unitary U, the eigenstates of U encode information about both P₁ and P₂. Each measurement outcome (0 or 1 on each qubit) maps to eigenvalues of both operators. After 1000 shots, you have enough statistics to compute ⟨P₁⟩ and ⟨P₂⟩ separately from the same data — like reading two answers from one exam.

Why can't you just group any Pauli strings together? Why specifically anticommuting ones? Because the math of combining into a valid unitary only works when terms cancel — and that cancellation requires anticommutation.
Step 1: The requirement
For strings P₁, P₂ to be combined, we need:
p₁*p₂ · P₁P₂ + p₂*p₁ · P₂P₁ = 0
The cross-terms in the combined operator must vanish. Otherwise the combined operator isn't a valid unitary.
Step 2a: If they ANTICOMMUTE (P₁P₂ = −P₂P₁)
p₁*p₂ · P₁P₂ + p₂*p₁ · P₂P₁
= p₁*p₂ · P₁P₂ + p₂*p₁ · (−P₁P₂)   ← substitute anticommute relation
= p₁*p₂ · P₁P₂ − p₂*p₁ · P₁P₂
= (p₁*p₂ − p₂*p₁) · P₁P₂
= 0   ✓ Terms cancel! Combination is valid.
The minus sign from anticommutation makes the two terms subtract to zero. The combined unitary is mathematically valid.
Step 2b: If they COMMUTE (P₁P₂ = +P₂P₁)
p₁*p₂ · P₁P₂ + p₂*p₁ · P₂P₁
= p₁*p₂ · P₁P₂ + p₂*p₁ · (+P₁P₂)   ← substitute commute relation
= p₁*p₂ · P₁P₂ + p₂*p₁ · P₁P₂
= (p₁*p₂ + p₂*p₁) · P₁P₂
= 2·Re(p₁*p₂) · P₁P₂ ≠ 0   ✗ Doesn't cancel! Combination is INVALID.
The plus sign means the terms add up instead of canceling. The resulting operator is not a valid unitary — using it would give wrong measurement results.
The sign flip is everything.

Anticommute: P₁P₂ = −P₂P₁ → cross terms subtract → zero → valid unitary ✓
Commute: P₁P₂ = +P₂P₁ → cross terms add → nonzero → invalid ✗

This one minus sign is the entire reason the paper builds an anticommutation graph, does clique partitioning, converts to graph coloring, and invents the Picasso algorithm. Everything traces back to this cancellation.
At small scale the savings seem modest. At real molecular scale, grouping is the difference between feasible and impossible.
Measurement rounds: without vs with grouping
Paper's results on real molecules:

H6_3D_sto3g: 8,721 strings → 901 unitaries (10.3× reduction)
H4_1D_631g: 42,449 strings → 2,943 unitaries (14.4× reduction)
H4_2D_6311g: 154,641 strings → 6,944 unitaries (22.3× reduction)
H6_2D_631g: 2,027,273 strings → ~300K unitaries (~6.8× reduction)

At 1,000 shots per measurement: H4_2D_6311g drops from 154 million shots to 6.9 million shots. That's the difference between weeks and hours on quantum hardware.
The full chain:

Anticommutation math → grouping is possible → graph coloring finds optimal groups → Picasso does this memory-efficiently → fewer unitaries → fewer measurements → quantum chemistry becomes tractable at scale.

Every piece of the paper exists to serve this one goal: reduce the number of measurement rounds by grouping Pauli strings into combined unitaries.
